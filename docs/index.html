Bauhaus (1919–1933)

Curator Card — Bauhaus (1919–1933) Founded in 1919 in Weimar by Walter Gropius, the Bauhaus was a German school of art, architecture, and design that operated in Weimar, Dessau, and Berlin until its closure in 1933 under political pressure from the National Socialist regime. The curriculum integrated fine arts, craft, and industrial production, emphasizing workshop-based instruction and functional design. Faculty included Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky, and László Moholy-Nagy. The school advanced principles of standardization, geometric abstraction, and material experimentation, contributing to modern architecture and industrial design. Its Dessau building (1925–26) exemplifies its architectural program. Vocabulary Tags: Functionalism Geometric abstraction Standardization Workshop pedagogy Industrial design Museum / Credited Collection Links: The Museum of Modern Art — https://www.moma.org Bauhaus Dessau Foundation — https://www.bauhaus-dessau.de Not to be confused with… the postwar “International Style,” which developed later and was influenced by, but distinct from, Bauhaus pedagogy and institutional structure.
