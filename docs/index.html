Bauhaus (1919–1933)

Curator Card — Bauhaus (1919–1933) Founded in 1919 in Weimar by Walter Gropius, the Bauhaus was a German school of art, architecture, and design that operated in Weimar, Dessau, and Berlin until its closure in 1933 under political pressure from the National Socialist regime. The curriculum integrated fine arts, craft, and industrial production, emphasizing workshop-based instruction and functional design. Faculty included Paul Klee, Wassily Kandinsky, and László Moholy-Nagy. The school advanced principles of standardization, geometric abstraction, and material experimentation, contributing to modern architecture and industrial design. Its Dessau building (1925–26) exemplifies its architectural program. Swiss Style / International Typographic Style (1950s–1970s)

Curator Card — Swiss Style / International Typographic Style (1950s–1970s) Developed in Switzerland in the 1950s, the Swiss Style—also termed the International Typographic Style—established a systematic approach to graphic design grounded in grid-based composition, asymmetric layout, and objective photography. Key practitioners included Josef Müller-Brockmann, Armin Hofmann, and Max Bill. The movement prioritized sans-serif typefaces such as Helvetica and Univers, standardized spacing, and typographic hierarchy to ensure clarity and reproducibility. Instruction at the Basel School of Design formalized its pedagogical framework. The style influenced corporate identity systems, editorial design, and public signage across Europe and the United States during the mid- to late twentieth century. New Wave Typography (late 1970s–1990s)

BODY: Emerging in the late 1970s and extending through the 1990s, New Wave Typography challenged the rationalist constraints of the International Typographic Style through layered composition, variable baselines, and expressive typographic structure. Associated with designers including Wolfgang Weingart, April Greiman, and Dan Friedman, the movement expanded grid systems rather than rejecting them, introducing spatial fragmentation, rule-breaking alignment, and photographic manipulation. Offset lithography and early digital tools supported experimentation with scale, rotation, and transparency. Instruction at the Basel School of Design influenced its development. New Wave informed postmodern graphic design discourse and publication design in Europe and the United States.

Tags: Layered composition · Variable baseline · Grid deconstruction · Photomechanical reproduction · Postmodern graphic design
